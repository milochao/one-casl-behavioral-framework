What is ONE CASL

CASL has one mission. We need one way of working.

CASL has served Asian Americans and immigrants in Chicago for more than 40 years. Our mission is to catalyze the transformation of individuals, families, and the community for an equitable future, building on the wisdom of generations. Our vision is of individuals, families, and communities who thrive in a diverse and inclusive world.

Today we work across four paths: Human Services, Education, Health, and Impact and Advocacy. We help more than 7,400 people each year. More than half of them use more than one CASL program. They deserve to feel like they are in one organization, not four separate ones. Whether they are talking to the first person at intake or the fifth person they have seen this month, the experience should feel the same: clear, kind, and followed through.

ONE CASL is our answer. It means one team, one way of working, across all four paths. It starts with five habits.

Naming what works is how we make sure everyone does it, not just some of us.

Our values and our habits

CASL has five values: Inclusivity, Empowerment, Collaboration, Transformation, and Resiliency. The 5 habits are how those values show up in your work every day. Each habit is connected to one value.

HABIT 01  ·  CARE

Care is what a person feels the moment they arrive.

Many of our clients have had difficult experiences before they walk in. They may have gone to another agency and been turned away. They may not speak English well. They may feel nervous or unsure.

The first thing they notice is how they are treated. Care happens in the moments before any program begins, before any form is signed, before any question is asked.

Care also means how we treat each other as colleagues. And how we treat ourselves. You cannot give others what you do not have.

LOOKS LIKE

Small things that make a real difference.

These are real actions. You can start any of them today.

In the space
+The room itself shows Care. Clean space. Somewhere to sit. Information posted in the languages your clients speak. The room says "you are welcome here" before anyone speaks.
With clients
+You say hello when someone walks in. You say their name if you know it. You tell them what will happen next. "Welcome to CASL. My name is ___. Here is what we will do today."
+Someone has been waiting 15 minutes. You walk over. You say their name. You let them know you have not forgotten them. You tell them how much longer the wait will be.
+You offer water. You sit down with them, not above them. You speak at a pace they can follow.
With colleagues
+A coworker has had a difficult day. You notice. You say something kind. You do not add to what they are already carrying.
For yourself
+Before a difficult meeting, you take a moment. You breathe. You arrive present, not rushed.
+When you are struggling, you say so. You ask for support. Asking for help is part of the job, not a sign of failure.
For managers
You notice when someone on your team is struggling, before they decide to leave. You run a brief check-in after difficult cases. You give your team what you want them to give their clients. The habit of Care starts with you.
Your action: After a difficult case or a busy hour, check in with your team before they move to the next thing. Ask: what do you need right now?
DOES NOT LOOK LIKE

These things happen. We are naming them so we can change them.

xA person sits in the lobby for 30 minutes without any staff speaking to them or telling them how long the wait will be. A form is handed over with no explanation. A staff member absorbs a very difficult story and then goes directly to the next meeting with no break.

HABIT 02  ·  COORDINATION

No one should have to tell their story twice.

CASL has four paths. Each path has different staff, different programs, and different systems. For a client, moving from one path to another can feel like starting over every time, with a new face and the same questions again.

Coordination is our answer to that. We share what we know about each client before we meet them. We walk with them to the next person. We make sure the next person is ready.

When Coordination works, a client feels like CASL is one place, not four.

LOOKS LIKE

You walk with them. You do not point.

With clients
+You know what every CASL path does. You can explain any of them in one sentence. You know who to call. You do not need to look it up while a client is waiting.
+When a client is moving to the next staff member, you go first. You say: "I want to introduce you to [name]. She will help you from here. I have told her about you." Then you bring the client over.
+Before your first meeting with a client, you read their intake record. You already know their situation. You open the meeting: "I have looked at your file. I know you already went through intake. Let us continue from there."
With colleagues
+A client could benefit from another CASL program. You do not just tell them. You make the connection yourself. You create the referral in the system. You tell the next person they are coming.
For managers
Your job includes the space between teams. When a client needs to move from one team to another, you make sure it happens. You do not wait for someone else to notice the gap. Run a regular coordination meeting — not a status meeting, but one built around this question: who needs to know what about which client? When the problem is between departments, you raise it. That is also your job.
Your action: In your next team meeting, ask one question: who is a client we are all currently working with, and does everyone who needs to know, know?
DOES NOT LOOK LIKE

When a client has to tell their story again, something went wrong.

xA client is given a room number and sent alone. They arrive and no one expected them. The same questions are asked that intake already answered.
xA client could benefit from another program. It is mentioned in the meeting. No connection is made, no referral is logged. Nothing happens.

Habit 03  ·  CLARITY
Clarity.
Always tell people what comes next.
For clients Plain words. The next step, always named. Check that they understood.
For colleagues Write it down. Name the next step. Do not assume.
For yourself Know what your job is. Know what it is not. Ask when you are not sure.
Habit 03  ·  CLARITY
What it looks like.
Simple words. Said plainly. In the language they speak.
Habit 03  ·  CLARITY
What it does not look like.
A person leaves without knowing what will happen next. That is a Clarity failure.
HABIT 03  ·  CLARITY

People can only take the next step if they know what it is.

Many of the people who come to CASL have been passed from agency to agency. At each stop, no one explained what was happening or why they had to sign a form. No one told them the answer was no, or what they could do instead.

We understand that. Clarity is our answer. When a person leaves CASL, they know exactly what will happen next.

Clarity also means confirming that people understood you. Saying it plainly is the first step. Asking "does that make sense?" is the second.

LOOKS LIKE

Say it plainly. Say it every time.

+"This form is so you do not have to explain yourself every time you come to CASL. We keep your information so our team already knows your situation." Then you ask: "Do you have any questions about that?"
+"After today, someone from benefits will call you by Thursday. If you do not hear from us by then, here is the number to call." You write it down for them.
+"You do not qualify for this program today. But here are two things we can do right now to help." You name both things. You do not leave it there.
+At the end of every meeting: "Your next step is ___. My next step is ___. We will speak again on ___." Then you ask: "Is that clear? Do you have any questions?"
For managers
Your staff know what is expected of them because you said it clearly. If your team has to guess what is expected by watching each other, the expectation has not been communicated. Write it down. Say it out loud. Then check that they understood.
Your action: Pick one expectation your team should know. Write it down. Say it at your next check-in. Then ask: is that clear?
DOES NOT LOOK LIKE

When a person leaves not knowing what comes next, we can do better.

xA form is given with "sign here" and no explanation. A person told they do not qualify leaves with no next step and no resource. They walk out with nothing to hold onto.

Commitment.

Do what you said you would do. Every time, not most of the time.

Habit 04  ·  COMMITMENT
Commitment.
Do what you said you would do. Every time, not most of the time.
For clients A specific date. A written record. A real follow-up call.
For colleagues If you said it, it gets done. If you cannot do it, you say so first.
For yourself Only promise what you can actually do. Say less. Do more.
Habit 04  ·  COMMITMENT
What it looks like.
You said it. You wrote it down. You did it.
Habit 04  ·  COMMITMENT
What it does not look like.
A client who was not followed up will likely not come back. And they will not send anyone else.
HABIT 04  ·  COMMITMENT

Trust is built one follow-through at a time.

When a person leaves CASL, they are often in a difficult situation. They are counting on us to do what we said. If we do not follow up, they do not come back. And they tell the people they know.

Commitment means every promise has a name, a date, and a record.

LOOKS LIKE

Written down. Logged. Done.

+"I will follow up with you by Thursday." You log it in the system before the meeting is over. The record is made before you leave the room.
+Before the first appointment in a program, the staff member calls to confirm. Not an automated message. A real call from a real person.
+Every referral is logged. If it is not in the system, it did not happen. You check that it happened.
+You told a colleague you would send something by 3pm. At 2:45, you either send it or you tell them you need more time. You do not go quiet.
+If the answer is "we cannot help you right now," that is also a commitment. You say when you can. You say what to do in the meantime. You name the date you will follow up. Then you do it.
For managers
You build the system so follow-through does not depend on each person's motivation alone. When everyone is busy, motivation is not enough. A note in a personal diary is not a system. A required field in Salesforce with a follow-up date is a system. You check that follow-ups happened. When they did not, you fix the system, not only the person.
Your action: This week, check that follow-ups from last week happened. If they did not, ask what got in the way — and fix that, not only the person.
DOES NOT LOOK LIKE

A promise with no name, no date, and no record is not a commitment.

xA person leaves with "someone will contact you." No name. No date. No follow-up. The referral mentioned in the meeting is never logged and never happens. The person waits. Nothing comes.

HABIT 05  ·  CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT

A mistake is only a problem if we repeat it.

When something goes wrong, the question is not who to blame. The question is: what do we do differently next time?

Speaking up can feel risky. We know that. We are working to make CASL a place where naming a problem is seen as a contribution, not a complaint. That takes time. We are building it on purpose.

Continuous Improvement is not a big project. It is a small habit. It is asking, after every interaction: what is one thing I can do better next time?

LOOKS LIKE

Small fixes. Said out loud. Made together.

+You bring a problem to your team meeting before it becomes a bigger issue. "Here is something that is not working. Here is what I think we should try."
+"Here is what I would do differently next time." Said without defensiveness. Said as something normal.
+The pilot showed something that did not work. You do not defend the original version. You change it.
+A client shares feedback, good or difficult. You write it down. You bring it to the team. You use it to make a change.
For managers
Your job is not just to say it is safe to speak up. Your job is to show it. You show it by what you do the first time someone names a problem. People learn quickly whether speaking up leads to change or to trouble. When someone names a problem, thank them. Then do something about it. When you do that, you build trust. That is how this habit takes root.
Your action: The next time someone on your team names a problem, thank them out loud, in front of the group. Then tell them what you will do about it, and when.
DOES NOT LOOK LIKE

Quiet is not the same as everything being fine.

xA workaround used for six months becomes the way things are done because no one named it as a problem. When we turn someone away, we do not record it, so we cannot see the pattern or change it.
All five habits together.

When all five habits are working, something real happens.

A client came to CASL after a serious accident. He had no stable housing, missed medical appointments, and personal belongings held by a former landlord. He was alone and did not know where to start.

The Illinois Welcoming Center was his first door. Within days, Behavioral Health, Legal Services, and a case worker were all working together around him. Eight case management sessions, no cost to him. By the end, he had his belongings back, his medical appointments completed, and a safe place to live.

That happened because staff coordinated across teams without being asked. Because someone made a commitment and followed through. Because no one sent him to another desk and left him to explain himself again.

That is ONE CASL. A way of working, every day, for every person.

KNOTS

When you see a habit, say something.

KNOTS is our way of recognizing each other. When you see someone practicing one of the 5 habits, you can give them a KNOT. A simple way of saying: I saw you do that. That was ONE CASL.

Recognition does not always come from the top. Sometimes the most powerful thing is when a coworker says: I noticed.

Your manager will share more about how to nominate someone. For now, just start noticing.

How it works
You see a colleague practice one of the 5 habits. You nominate them. They receive a KNOT named for that habit.
Who can nominate
Anyone on staff. Peer to peer. Manager to staff. Staff to manager. In any direction.
What a KNOT means
It means someone saw you living ONE CASL. Not a performance review. A real moment, recognized by a real person.
